## TL;DR — The Play

1. **Pass on the paid ad for now** — but keep the door open for the future. "Advertised in HOA" carries little trust transfer (readers know it's paid); "Recommended by HOA" is far stronger. Trade the weak badge for a shot at the strong one.
2. **Go for the endorsement via "try the app."** Get Katerina to use a free Pro account and give honest feedback; let the endorsement follow naturally if she likes it. Don't ask her to vouch cold.
3. **Lock the affiliate deal** when the moment's right — zero cost, zero risk to HOA, performance-based. The perfect pre-PMF partnership.
4. **Do NOT buy the $1,000 conference sponsorship yet.** All-in cost (driving from Orlando + hotel + ~1 week of founder time) is ~$2,000–2,500. That's a *scale* spend, and we have nothing to scale yet (0 subs). Make the October conference the **reward for hitting traction**, not the bet we place at zero.
5. **Spend the next ~3 months getting our first ~50 paying subs** on cheap, trackable channels. Re-evaluate the conference and the ad by late August.

*(The $225/$300 ad analysis below is retained for reference — revisit once we have traction. Current decision: pass for now.)*

## The "Advertised In" Badge — name & logo rights

This is the durable value of the ad, so nail it down.

- **Safe floor (even with zero permission):** Plain-text **"Advertised in Homesteaders of America Magazine"** is a truthful statement about a paid placement (nominative fair use). Lower-risk than "As seen in," which leans toward implying editorial coverage.
- **Never use without explicit endorsement:** "Recommended by / Endorsed by / Approved by HOA." That's false advertising, not fair use.
- **Logo** requires permission. Plain text name does not, but their stylized mark does.
- **Relationship caveat:** Even though a truthful "advertised in" line is probably defensible, **if Katerina says don't use our name, don't.** A $225 badge isn't worth burning the partner. The legal right and the smart move aren't the same here.
- **Apple note:** The App Store may ask for proof/permission for third-party names and can reject anything implying endorsement. The listing is a higher bar than our own website.

**The ask (frame as the easy-yes factual version):**
> "Are you OK with us noting 'Advertised in Homesteaders of America Magazine' (plain text, no logo) on our website? Happy to skip it if you'd prefer." If they grant the logo too — even better.

*(Not legal advice — get anything they push back on in writing.)*

## Getting Katerina to Try the App → the Endorsement

The endorsement is the real prize. A recommendation from HOA borrows the trust we can't build alone. But you don't ask someone to vouch for an app sight-unseen — you get them to *use it first*.

### Why "try it and give feedback" is the right ask
- It's a **low-commitment ask** ("tell me what you think") rather than a high-commitment one ("put your name behind us").
- It naturally **leads to an endorsement** if she likes it — she's vouching for something she's actually touched.
- It signals confidence in the product and respect for her judgment — fitting for a bootstrapped team that would "rather earn the endorsement than ask for it cold."

### How to make trying it frictionless
- Send a **direct demo link / App Store link** + offer to set up a **free Pro account** so she sees the full experience (no paywall friction).
- Offer a **2-minute walkthrough** (call or short video) so she gets to the "aha" fast instead of poking around cold.
- Ask for **honest feedback first** — position the endorsement as "only if you genuinely think it's worth putting the HOA name behind."

### Endorsement forms to offer (define what "endorsement" means)
"Endorsement" is vague — give her easy, concrete options:
- A short written quote / testimonial we can use.
- A **member promo code** ("HOA20") — a *soft* pseudo-endorsement: makes her readers feel special, gives us tracking, and doesn't require her to formally vouch.
- A social post or newsletter mention (this is where the 150k / 70k reach lives).
- "HOA Recommended" wording + logo (the strongest, needs explicit sign-off).

### The fallback ladder
If she won't endorse:
1. Secure **name-use rights** for the "advertised in" badge (makes the ad worth it regardless).
2. Lock the **affiliate deal** (independent of endorsement — costs her nothing, she earns a share of subs she drives).
3. Pursue **editorial / product-feature** coverage (gift guides, "tools we love" roundups) — more credible than a paid ad, sometimes free.

## The Conference — park it, don't buy it yet

**The opportunity:** HOA Annual Conference, **Oct 8–10, Front Royal, VA**. 6,000+ attendees, 150+ vendors, sells out before gates open, plus an online audience.

**Wood Shop Sponsorship — $1,000:** sponsorship of 2026 event · 4 weekend passes · 10×10 vendor space (table + 2 chairs) · quarter-page ad in HOA program · logo on website.

**Why it's tempting:** It absorbs the magazine ad (program ad included), gives credibility-by-association (logo + sponsor status + likely backlink), and lets us put the app in homesteaders' hands live — the highest-converting thing for a "track your animals" app.

**Why NOT yet:**
- **All-in cost ≈ $2,000–2,500**, not $1,000 (driving Orlando↔Front Royal ~800 mi each way, ~$200 gas, 4–5 nights hotel ~$600–900, food ~$300). Plus **~a full week of founder time** — the most expensive line item pre-PMF.
- **We have 0 subs.** A conference is a *scale* move — you pour money into a funnel you've already proven converts. We haven't proven anyone pays at $5.99/$14.99, don't know trial→paid %, and don't know churn. Spending $2.5k to *discover* this is the wrong first dollar.
- **Booth ROI math:** recouping ~$2.5k all-in at $5.99 needs ~400+ *retained* subscriber-months. Pulling that from one event when we've never converted a single payer is optimistic.

**Decision rule (it's ~4 months out):**
- Spend the next 3 months getting to **first ~50 paying subs** on cheap, trackable channels.
- **If by late August** we have real conversion + retention signal → the conference becomes a justified scale move. Go, sponsor, demo.
- **If still ~0** → skip the sponsorship. Optionally drive up as a **regular attendee** for a couple days to validate face-to-face, then come back next year as a sponsor *with testimonials and known unit economics*.

**If/when we do sponsor, ask:**
1. Is the Wood Shop tier still available? (Sells out early.)
2. Do sponsors get the attendee/lead list for follow-up?
3. Any virtual sponsor benefit for the online audience?
4. Can we use "Proud sponsor of HOA 2026" + logo in our marketing?
5. How long does the website logo/backlink stay up, and is it a followed link?

**App-booth strategy (it's an app, not a product on a table):** big screen/tablet running a live demo · QR standee → download + conference-only trial/discount · raffle/giveaway for email capture · **offline-capable demo + own hotspot** (rural VA wifi/cell will be unreliable — a Firebase app that won't load at the booth is a disaster).

## The 0→1 Plan (what to actually do now)

We're spending *scale* money on *discovery* work if we lead with the conference. Two different jobs:
- **0→1 (discovery):** cheap, fast, iterative channels where we talk to users and fix the funnel. Goal: learn *if and why* people pay.
- **1→100 (scale):** pour money into a funnel we've already proven.

**Next ~3 months:**
1. **First 20–50 paying subs from cheap channels** — homesteading Facebook groups, r/homestead, small $50–100 Meta/Reddit ad tests, ASO, a bit of content/SEO. Trackable and iterable.
2. **Nail the numbers** — trial→paid %, which price wins ($5.99 vs $14.99), month-1 churn, rough LTV.
3. **Talk to 15–20 real homesteaders** about why they did/didn't pay. Nearly free, and the feedback we most need right now.

Once we know *"for every X visitors I get Y payers worth $Z,"* we can spend on the conference (or scale ads) with confidence.
